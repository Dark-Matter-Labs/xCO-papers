An overview of the work

The xCO options portfolio, in five domains

A clear map of the work xCO is building: five systemic risks facing societies, and for each one a portfolio of response — strategic positions and concrete options already underway on the ground.

xCO turns cascading civilizational risk into investable portfolios of response — organised into five domains, each holding strategic positions and anchored by live work already underway on the ground.

Core communication device

The logic chain

How abstract existential risk becomes an investable response portfolio. This is the diagram for decks, the website, and funder briefings.

1. Volatility rises

Biophysical, technological, ecological and social shocks intensify.

2. Risks cascade

Heat, water, food, housing, health, violence, infrastructure and trust interact.

3. Optionality is spent

The set of futures a system can still reach narrows — before the harm is visible.

4. Institutions fall short

Most were built for single problems, not risks that cross many domains and actors at once.

5. xCO maps the response

It identifies where the risks are, who they hit, and where a response could take hold and spread.

6. Portfolios form

Each domain combines concrete options, shared capabilities and capital into one coordinated response.

7. Capacity returns

Places and institutions regain the ability to respond — and to keep responding to whatever comes next.

Why timing is everything: the window closes early

The chance to act on a risk usually disappears before its consequences become visible. A coastal town can become uninsurable — and therefore unsellable — long before any flood arrives. A region loses its ability to adapt to water shortage once the groundwater is gone, not when the first harvest fails. By the time the damage shows, the choices that could have prevented it have often already closed. So xCO works from the early warning signs, while there is still a decision to make.

Underneath all five

A shared set of capabilities every domain draws on — risk‑sensing, coordination, legal instruments, media and learning — and a way for different kinds of capital to take part. Both are set out in the two sections that follow.

The deeper goal

What each option really builds

Every option begins with a concrete, near‑term goal — cool Madrid by around 7.5°C, stabilise a watershed, hold a bioregion in balance. The goal is real and necessary, and it is what makes the larger work politically and financially possible. But it is the entry point, not the end.

The goal

A concrete mission goal space — specific enough to mobilise politics, partners and funding around a clear target, and to show where abstract risk touches the continuity of everyday life.

The real product

Systemic capability. Cooling a city means citizens cooling and greening streets, distributed sensing, and coordination between people and institutions that did not exist before — the same capability the place will need for the next shock, and the one after.

The horizon

Civilizational optionality. Built in one place and spread to the next, these capabilities widen the range of futures societies can still reach. The concrete goal wins the permission; the capability is the real product; keeping futures open is the point.

How the work is done matters as much as whether it is done

Cooling a city in a way that concentrates power is a failure even if the temperature falls. The way a place is cooled or rebuilt determines the political economy that results: a centralised programme creates dependency; a distributed one builds civic agency, local fabrication, maintenance cultures and plural ownership. The aim is not efficiency alone — it is to expand future capacity.

This is also why xCO does not simply describe collapse. Fear alone shuts people down. Each domain shows a credible, fundable field of response — so capital and communities can act on risk they are already exposed to, rather than look away.

The reusable layer

The capabilities every domain draws on

The most distinctive thing xCO builds is not any single option — it is a reusable sequence of capabilities that turns a sensed risk into action on the ground, and lets what works in one place be rebuilt elsewhere, at speed.

1. Risk sensing

Reading the early signs that a place is losing its room to act.

2. Desire construction

Building appetite for a better future — through media, campaigns and culture — so change is wanted, not imposed.

3. Demand construction

Turning that into structured demand — financial signal, political mandate, civic legitimacy — and designing the portfolio of interventions that meets it.

4. Capital orchestration

Matching the right kind of capital to the right part of the work.

5. Financial facility

The vehicle that holds and deploys capital in place — e.g. a city cooling bank.

6. Outcome accelerator

Where capital meets demand and many actors build and test real responses together — without a new monopoly forming at the centre.

7. Learning system

Capturing what works so it can travel and be rebuilt elsewhere.

Underpinning the sequence throughout: the new legal and financial instruments — outcome facilities, risk pools, fiduciary trusts — that let value flow where existing structures cannot hold it.

For capital partners

How capital takes part

The binding constraint is not money, technology or expertise — it is the architecture that turns shared risk exposure into coordinated commitments. Capital takes part through three pools, each with a different job and return logic. The structure is still in formation; the figures below are indicative.

1

Systemic risk-bearing capital

Non-returnable. Seeds the deal construction that builds each option — roughly £50k–£10m per option, around three options per viable position.

2

Tooling fund

Risk-on. Builds the new products, services and capabilities needed to arrange and run executions across the portfolio.

3

First-right-to-invest fund

Returnable. Puts the first commercial capital — roughly the first £10m — into each option's own financial facility, such as a Madrid Cooling Bank.

For any allocator with systemic exposure, this is not risk‑on capital making a bet. It is risk‑in capital — already exposed to cascade events through every other asset class — finally having an instrument to act on that exposure.

Mission return

Domain 01 — Planetary breakdown

The risk here is irreversible: once these Earth systems cross their thresholds, the futures they support close for good. The domain covers the cryosphere (Alpine, Himalayan, Antarctic and Arctic ice that holds sea level, ocean currents and regional climate in place), ocean chemistry and marine life, atmospheric rivers, and the densest reservoirs of biodiversity. The response works to hold these systems above the line beyond which there is no return.

The furthest‑developed proposition is the One‑percent planetary trust — an effort to protect roughly one percent of the world's most critical biodiversity and land, the reservoir of evolutionary potential through which life retains the capacity to adapt (the Amazon and the Congo are subsets, not the whole). It is designed to run through a dual architecture: the trust holding strategic, long‑horizon responsibility, while local stewardship compacts hold operational custodianship, so planetary preservation and local livelihoods coexist without collapsing into centralised control. Alongside it, work is developing on Antarctic stabilisation and on marine regeneration in the North Sea and with Whale Nation.

Domain 02 — Failing territories

The risk is that whole regions become unable to feed, water and house the people who live in them — soils exhausted, aquifers drained, food chains stretched thin, land enclosed. The response is place‑based: holding a whole living region in balance — soils, water, food and people as one interlocking system rather than a stack of sectors — so it stays habitable and productive under pressure.

Much of the live work runs through RZ Studio: Findhorn (regenerative agriculture, including salmon farming) is decided and in place; Muga Valley, Tayside, an ecological land trust and Tastée are developing; and Dartmoor is at an early stage. Terra‑region (the Alps) is developing through a separate partnership, and Santiago sits here too, as a question of glacier water and regional viability. Positions include regenerative agriculture (soil‑deep, water‑retentive, low‑input biological production), nutritional sovereignty — securing nutrition closer to home so a price shock anywhere is not a health crisis everywhere — and self‑sovereign land, holding land in common trust across generations rather than letting it concentrate into dispossession.

Domain 03 — Unliveable cities

The risk is that extreme heat and brittle, extractive construction push cities past the point where ordinary life can continue — with health, the economy and social cohesion failing together. The guiding principle of the response is that every intervention should do several jobs at once: an urban forest should cool the city, recharge its aquifers, produce food, create habitat and give residents something to tend — all at the same time.

The Madrid Cooling Coalition is developing fast (cooling a city already hitting 47°C is an organisational problem of coordinating thousands of actors, not only a technical one), and Copenhagen is developing the zero‑extraction pathway — building without virgin material extraction, where construction accounts for roughly 40% of global emissions, so new supply chains, instruments and demand have to be built at once. Birmingham is at an earlier stage on the three‑degree neighbourhoods work — rethinking cooling, water, materials, energy and social infrastructure together at the scale of the street.

Domain 04 — Authoritarian futures

The risk is that, under mounting pressure, the demand for strong government is met by coercion: emergency powers that never lapse, plural debate narrowing into control. The response builds civic power that is materially consequential without becoming a new coercive centre of its own — legally empowered, financially resourced, locally grounded and able to coordinate across scales.

Its positions are mutually reinforcing: civic resilience & rapid response (neighbourhood and municipal resilience, mutual aid and community‑owned lifeline systems, plus lawful shock absorbers that mobilise in hours), legal power & accountability (litigation, investigative assemblies and registries that turn grievance into reform rather than rupture), decision‑making & civic finance — the infrastructure for making contested collective choices and resourcing them, developed with the World Bank around the idea of soft civic assets as strategic assets — and democratic capacity & deliberation, treated as a contractible, investable asset class including instruments such as the democracy bond.

This work is carried by the Heart Power portfolio (UK), a vehicle that seeds and connects civic options so they are stronger together than alone — built with the people who will hold them.

Domain 05 — The crisis of being human

The risk is that, as AI absorbs more of what people were paid and valued for, human worth, agency and livelihood quietly erode — and the window to set the foundations differently is narrow.

This is the earliest domain, taking shape as three early typologies: the future of being human (what value and dignity mean in this new settlement), human–machine relationships, and the collective intelligence of a city. Alongside them sits work on cultural and universal imagination: building desire for new futures, lived at city scale, through art and culture — so people want a different future and help make it, rather than merely endure it.

Shared vocabulary

A few terms

The language used throughout this overview, in plain form.

Domain

One of the five systemic risks the portfolio addresses — e.g. unliveable cities, or authoritarian futures. Each domain groups the pathways of response to that risk.

Position

A pathway of response to a domain's risk — e.g. urban cooling or bioregional regeneration. Can contain multiple options.

Option

A concrete intervention in a specific site or vehicle — e.g. the Madrid Cooling Coalition, or the one‑percent planetary trust.

Maturity stage

How far an option has travelled: Exploring (internal, no partners), Developing (co‑commitment with partners), or Live (allocation made in place).

Mission goal space

A concrete, near‑term target (such as cooling a city by several degrees) that mobilises politics and funding, and opens the deeper work behind it.

Systemic capability

The durable capacity an option builds in a place or field — coordination, instruments, civic infrastructure — that outlasts the original crisis.

Civilizational optionality

The structural ability of societies to coordinate under stress and keep multiple futures open. What xCO exists to expand.

Risk‑in capital

Capital already exposed to cascading risk through every other asset class — now able to act on that exposure directly.
